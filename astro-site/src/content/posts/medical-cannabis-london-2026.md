## Is medical cannabis actually legal in the UK?

Yes. The law changed on 1 November 2018, moving cannabis-based products for medicinal use from Schedule 1 (no medical value) to Schedule 2 (recognised medical value) under the Misuse of Drugs Regulations 2001.

The practical effect: GMC-registered specialist doctors can now prescribe cannabis-based medicinal products when they believe it is clinically appropriate. Your GP cannot prescribe it — only a specialist on the General Medical Council register, which is why every patient pathway runs through a private clinic or, in rare cases, an NHS specialist.

The law is the same in London as it is in Manchester, Birmingham, or anywhere else in the UK — there is no "London exception" that makes access easier or harder here.

## How eligibility works

UK clinics assess patients against a consistent framework. You generally need:

1. **A diagnosed, ongoing condition** — chronic pain, anxiety, insomnia, PTSD, multiple sclerosis, epilepsy, fibromyalgia, migraine, IBD, chemotherapy-related nausea, and others are commonly assessed
2. **Evidence of prior treatments** — typically two or more standard licensed treatments tried without adequate relief or with intolerable side effects
3. **Access to your medical records** — your GP Summary Care Record documents your diagnosis and treatment history
4. **To be 18 or over** — adult clinics assess adults; paediatric access runs through NHS specialists

## What the first appointment looks like

Your initial consultation will almost certainly be a video call — you do not need to travel anywhere. Here is what to expect:

1. **Before the appointment:** Request your GP Summary Care Record (free — ask your practice reception or use the NHS App under Profile → GP health record). Upload it to the clinic's patient portal.
2. **The consultation:** 30–60 minute video call with a GMC-registered specialist. They review your medical history, discuss your condition, and assess whether cannabis-based treatment is appropriate. Be honest — they are doctors, not gatekeepers.
3. **The outcome:** If prescribed, the specialist writes a prescription sent to a pharmacy. Medication typically arrives by courier within 1–5 days. If not prescribed, they explain why and what would need to change.
4. **Follow-ups:** Usually every 1–3 months initially, then spreading to quarterly or six-monthly once stable.

## London-specific considerations

**Pharmacy delivery:** Most prescriptions are dispensed by specialist pharmacies and delivered by courier. London addresses typically receive next-day delivery. Some pharmacies offer same-day courier within the M25 — check with your clinic.

**Transport and driving:** The DVLA's position on cannabis-based medicines is that patients should not drive if impaired, the same as any prescribed medicine that affects driving ability. Follow your clinician's advice and the information leaflet that accompanies your medication. London's public transport network means driving is optional for most — a practical advantage for London patients.

**Employment:** London employers range from progressive to conservative on medical cannabis. NHS and civil service employers have specific policies. Private sector varies. Your clinic can provide a treatment letter for HR if needed. You are not required to disclose your medication to your employer, though some regulated professions (pilots, train drivers, some healthcare roles) have mandatory reporting requirements.

**Private health insurance:** Some London-centric insurers and employer health plans now cover at least the consultation cost. Check your policy — Bupa, AXA, and Vitality have updated their positions in 2025–2026. If your employer provides private medical insurance through a London-based scheme, it is worth asking.
