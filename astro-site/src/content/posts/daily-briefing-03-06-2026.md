<p>Good evening. Here's what's moving in the world of medical cannabis today.</p>

<h2>Kentucky Governor expands medical cannabis access via executive order</h2>
<p>Governor Andy Beshear has issued an executive order expanding medical cannabis access in Kentucky. The emergency regulation is intended to broaden patient eligibility and streamline access to the state's medical programme.</p>
<p>"This emergency regulation will help more Kentuckians reclaim their lives through safe, nonaddictive medical cannabis," Governor Beshear said in a statement reported by NORML. The order takes immediate effect, though full details of which patient groups or conditions are newly covered have yet to be published by the state.</p>

<h2>New York medical cannabis operators struggling with millions in state fees</h2>
<p>Medical cannabis operators in New York state owe millions of dollars in conversion fees to the state, which they claim they cannot afford. The fees, set at $15 million per operator, were designed to transition existing medical licence holders into the state's broader adult-use market.</p>
<p>Operators are now pleading for a break, arguing that current market conditions — including falling wholesale prices and a saturated retail environment — render the fee unviable. The state's Office of Cannabis Management has yet to respond publicly to the requests for financial relief.</p>

<h2>US federal agency sets standards for cannabinoid medicine in value-based care</h2>
<p>The US Centres for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) is establishing new standards for what cannabinoid medicine looks like within its Beneficiary Engagement Incentive programme. The initiative is not a broad cannabis reimbursement scheme, but rather a clinician-led pathway for hemp-derived products in value-based care models.</p>
<p>The move signals a cautious but notable step by US federal health authorities toward integrating cannabinoid-based treatments into mainstream healthcare reimbursement structures, albeit limited to hemp-derived products at this stage.</p>

<h2>Vermont lawmakers advance bill doubling adult-use possession limits</h2>
<p>Lawmakers in Vermont have sent a bill to the governor that would double the state's adult-use cannabis possession limits. Under current law, possessing up to one ounce of cannabis is legal, but possessing between one and two ounces is classified as a criminal misdemeanour, punishable by up to six months in jail and a $500 fine.</p>
<p>The proposed legislation would raise the legal possession threshold to two ounces, effectively decriminalising what is currently a low-level criminal offence. The bill now awaits the governor's signature or veto.</p>
