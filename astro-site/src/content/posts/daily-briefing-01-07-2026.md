<p>Good evening. Here's what's moving in the world of medical cannabis today.</p>

<h2><a href="https://norml.org/blog/2026/07/01/analysis-drug-related-arrests-disciplinary-incidents-on-college-campuses-decline-following-marijuana-legalization/">Analysis: Drug-related arrests and disciplinary incidents on US college campuses decline following marijuana legalisation</a></h2>
<p>A new analysis published by researchers at Montana State University and the University of Oregon has found that state-level legalisation of recreational cannabis is associated with significant reductions in drug-related arrests and disciplinary incidents on college campuses. The study, covered by NORML, reports that decreases in arrests were particularly steep at public institutions and four-year universities.</p>
<p>The findings add to a growing body of evidence suggesting that legalisation shifts enforcement priorities and reduces the burden of the criminal justice system on young adults. The authors concluded that "state legalisation of recreational marijuana substantially reduced the arrests and disciplinary incidents for drug law violations."</p>

<h2><a href="https://norml.org/blog/2026/07/06/why-i-chose-cannabis-over-opioids/">Why I chose cannabis over opioids: a personal account</a></h2>
<p>In a personal essay published by NORML, summer intern Dakota Helgren has shared her reasons for substituting cannabis for opioids to manage chronic pain. The piece offers a firsthand perspective on the therapeutic potential of cannabinoids as an alternative to conventional pain management.</p>
<p>Helgren's account reflects a broader trend among patients in the United States, where opioid-related deaths remain a public health crisis. The essay highlights the role of medical cannabis in providing pain relief without the risk of respiratory depression or dependence associated with opioid medications.</p>

<h2><a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cwyd1l0lge8o?at_medium=RSS&at_campaign=rss">Over one million children referred for mental healthcare in England, with anxiety the main reason</a></h2>
<p>New data from the BBC reveals that more than one million children in England have been referred for mental healthcare, with anxiety identified as the primary condition driving demand. Services are struggling to keep pace, leaving many young people waiting years for treatment.</p>
<p>This surge in referrals comes as campaigners and clinicians continue to call for expanded access to alternative therapies, including medical cannabis, for treatment-resistant anxiety and other paediatric mental health conditions. The data underscores the growing pressure on the NHS to find effective, scalable solutions.</p>
